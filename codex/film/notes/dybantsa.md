## Frame-based observations (mine)

- Jump shot, elevated release. On a left-wing jumper vs Abilene Christian (frame_0544.jpg, ball flight in frame_0547.jpg), the ball leaves above and slightly in front of his head with full arm extension at the top of the jump. The release point sits well above the closest defender's contest. Frames cannot show the rhythm or speed of the load, only that the apex release is high.
- Free throw setup, squared and narrow-calm base (frame_0549.jpg, frame_0550.jpg, frame_0551.jpg). Close-ups at the line show shoulders level, eyes fixed on the rim, and a relaxed neutral posture before the dip. The broadcast framing crops the lower body, so base width at the line cannot be fully judged from these frames.
- Finishing extension at the rim (frame_0302.jpg through frame_0304.jpg). Sequence vs California Baptist, he attacks from the right wing (frame_0302) and two frames later is fully extended at the rim with the ball above the cylinder (frame_0304), body long and balanced rather than crashing sideways. The frames support full one-hand extension above the rim; they cannot establish how quickly he got there.
- Body frame in game context (frame_0903.jpg, frame_0551.jpg). Close-ups vs Utah and at the FT line show broad, square shoulders on a lean torso with long arms relative to jersey fit, a wing build with room for mass in the chest and arms rather than an already-filled-out frame.
- Handle posture under pressure (frame_1200.jpg, frame_0300.jpg). Vs No. 6 Iowa State he is crouched into a low attacking posture with the ball on his right hip and his shoulders over his knees; vs California Baptist (frame_0300) he operates from a staggered stance at the top of the key with the ball low and outside his frame. Frames show a low, shielded handle posture, not the speed of the moves themselves.
- What the frames cannot support, no claims here about burst, first-step speed, defensive timing, or feel; the 1 fps sampling skips the actual movement between positions.

## Sourced scouting observations (human scouts, cited)

- ESPN's Tim Bontemps and Jeremy Woo compared Dybantsa to a "supercharged Jaylen Brown" on the high end and a "supercharged RJ Barrett" on the low end, per coverage of ESPN scouting comps. Source, Deseret News, https://www.deseret.com/sports/2026/06/02/aj-dybantsa-2026-nba-draft-player-comparisons-espn/ (referencing ESPN).
- Scouting consensus describes him as an elite three-level scorer and arguably the best isolation and transition player in the class, an explosive 6'9" wing who plays above the rim and finishes through contact, but notes his perimeter jumper must become more consistent for the tough-shot diet to translate. Source, Babcock Hoops scouting report, https://www.babcockhoops.com/post/2026-nba-draft-aj-dybantsa-scouting-report
- HoopsHype's draft intel flags that his defensive engagement can waver, with lapses off-ball or while carrying a heavy scoring load, while noting wings with his tools and competitiveness tend to become positive defenders over time. Source, HoopsHype, https://www.hoopshype.com/story/sports/nba/draft/2026/05/10/aj-dybantsa-nba-draft-scouting-report-and-intel/89755882007/
