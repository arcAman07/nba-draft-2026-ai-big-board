## Frame-based observations (mine)

- Body frame/build: the broadcast close-ups frame_0013.jpg, frame_0015.jpg (Texas Tech game, white #4) and frame_0253.jpg (Iowa State game, road black) show a visibly lean guard, narrow shoulders, thin arms, very little upper-body bulk. The jersey hangs loose off his frame in both games; he is one of the slighter-built players in any frame he shares.
- Isolation handle posture: in the Sweet 16 sequence frame_0195.jpg, frame_0196.jpg he sizes up Illinois' #2 at the top of the key with knees bent and the ball worked low and wide of his body on the crossover; his chest stays over his base rather than leaning back, and his eyes are up at the defender in 0196.
- Shooting motion (one sequence captured): the same possession runs frame_0195.jpg through frame_0199.jpg. By frame_0197.jpg he is upright with his shooting arm fully extended overhead, elbow above ear, a one-armed finish consistent with a held follow-through after release; frame_0198.jpg (overhead camera) shows the ball in flight and frame_0199.jpg shows it dropping through the net on the rim camera. The camera angle does not allow a read of his set point or base width on this attempt.
- Scoring context the frames can verify: the on-screen graphic in frame_0092.jpg reads "4 KINGSTON FLEMINGS, 27 PTS | 4-5 3-PT" mid-game vs Texas Tech, confirming both his jersey number (4) and a high-efficiency three-point night in that game.
- Drive posture: frame_0058.jpg and frame_0135.jpg (Texas Tech) show him attacking into a crowded paint with a low center of gravity, shoulders below the defenders' chests, ball shielded on the far side.
- Limits of this sample: most frames in both clips are wide broadcast angles; I could not isolate a second close shooting motion, so set-point height, base width, and follow-through consistency across attempts cannot be assessed from these frames. Nothing here speaks to his speed or burst, which frames cannot show.

## Sourced scouting observations (human scouts, cited)

- FanSided/Yahoo Sports ("Kingston Flemings is lightning in a bottle") calls him "the fastest player in the 2026 draft... shot out of a cannon on every possession," and praises how he "blends this electric speed with incredible processing and live-dribble passing," noting a 26.5 usage rate against a modest 13.7 turnover rate. Source, https://fansided.com/nba/nba-draft-scouting-report-kingston-flemings-is-lightning-in-a-bottle (also at https://sports.yahoo.com/articles/nba-draft-scouting-report-kingston-171908273.html)
- NBADraft.net's scouting report flags the jumper, "shooting mechanics include extra motion and could be streamlined for efficiency... release can resemble a slight sling from the shoulder," and notes he is a sub-80% free-throw shooter, below ideal for an NBA lead guard. Source, https://www.nbadraft.net/players/kingston-flemings/
- Babcock Hoops views him as "arguably the best pure point guard prospect" in the class and "probably the best defender of the lottery point guards," citing elite stock numbers for a guard (3.0 STL%, 1.3 BLK%), while cautioning that his "skinny frame needs to keep filling out." Source, https://www.babcockhoops.com/post/2026-nba-draft-kingston-flemings-scouting-report
- Floor & Ceiling projects his range "likely to start around five and end around ten" and emphasizes there "isn't a better rim pressure guard in the class." Source, https://floorandceiling.substack.com/p/houston-flemings-scouting-report
